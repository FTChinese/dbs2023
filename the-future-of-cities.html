The future of cities

A new way of living

Why tackling climate change and rapid urbanisation requires a smarter approach to urban design

Scroll

As you sip coffee and watch the morning sun move across Riyadh from one of the

50 sky bridges

in the King Abdullah Financial District (KAFD), the city begins to buzz with life. Elevators zip up and down, transporting sporty commuters to the running paths overhead as sprinklers water the vertical gardens running down the sides of the city’s tall glass facades.

Even the low rumble of traffic that once accompanied your morning commute has disappeared. Cities feel different in the 2040s.

But when did the urban world begin to change?

Accommodating more people requires the interaction of local and long-distance solutions

Between 2018 and 2050, urban populations are expected to increase by roughly

2.5 billion

Accommodating more people without clogging up cities requires a smarter approach to urban design.

“When urban migration increases, cities tend to sprawl outwards,” explains Sabah Barakat, acting CEO of the PIF-owned New Murabba Development Company (NMDC), which is in charge of a huge downtown area to be developed in Riyadh. “This pushes residential areas further away from the jobs and services in the city centre, which then creates congestion as more people need to travel in and out.”

To alleviate this congestion, PIF is investing in vast urban developments to create societies where people can live, work and play without travelling – in the case of the expansive KAFD – more than 10 minutes from their home.

In New Murabba, Riyadh’s futuristic downtown development, residential areas will not be organised into blocks but built around ‘mobility loops’ – cycle paths or walkways that allow you to take your children to school on foot or get to the cinema on an e-bike in less than 15 minutes.

Reducing bottlenecks at the heart of major cities poses another challenge. The solution is to think in terms of three dimensions. “In KAFD we are building 50 sky bridges that not only make better use of vertical space, but help people move across a climate-controlled walkable urban environment in which all necessary amenities are within 10 minutes of home and work,” explains Gautam Sashittal, CEO of KAFD DMC.

By 2030, the KAFD will have around 6,100 five star hotel rooms and branded apartments

These human-centric solutions are key to PIF’s plans to help push Riyadh into the top 10 city economies in the world by 2030, but they are also developing a much longer-term model for urban life.

By 2030, the KAFD will have around 6,100 five star hotel rooms and branded apartments

NEOM, a giga-project owned by PIF, is envisioning a blueprint for cities over the next 150 years. Stretching

170km

through the desert, from the mountains of NEOM to the Red Sea, THE LINE will be the world’s first cognitive city, using AI and other technologies to give people a better quality of modern life.

Unlike other urban infrastructure projects, THE LINE is a greenfield site, which means the physical and digital architecture can be built in parity with people’s lives. The city’s first citizens, for example, will be moving into homes they have decorated online, walked through in virtual reality and built to their exact specifications.

By placing technology at the heart of urban design, PIF is adopting the best global practices in planning, implementation and innovation. This not only improves people’s standard of living, but ensures the sustainability of urban life.

Making these cities sustainable requires smarter use of the world around them

Human-centric solutions are key to PIF’s plans to help push Riyadh into the top 10 city economies in the world by 2030

As urban populations grow, they will also face increasingly extreme conditions. In a worst-case climate scenario, temperatures across the Middle East and North Africa could rise by as much as three degrees Celsius, putting pressure on urban populations and infrastructure.

11 per cent of WAREFA, ROSHN’s second community development in Riyadh, will be devoted to open and green spaces

Many of the solutions exist in the natural environment, and technology is helping many PIF companies determine how cities can make better use of the world around them. Planting more vegetation in the Wadi river basin, for example, could increase wind flow into Riyadh and lower temperatures naturally by as much as 5C. KAFD has also been designed in consideration of its surroundings, with a central pedestrian walkway connecting all areas, which was constructed below street level to capture wind flow, and ensure that temperatures are 8-10C cooler than central Riyadh.

Yet planting too much greenery in a very low humidity environment can put the water system under greater strain. Sensors and artificial intelligence are essential to determining where vegetation will be effective without being resource intensive.

11 per cent of WAREFA, ROSHN’s second community development in Riyadh, will be devoted to open and green spaces

People think a smart city is about the technology itself, but it’s more about how that technology facilitates smart planning,”

explains Barakat, pointing out how 3D models can be used to determine where buildings should be positioned to provide shade for those behind them, and where vegetation can shade walkways without blocking out natural light completely.

Smart planning is also evident at the integrated communities built by ROSHN, the PIF giga-project developing more than 400,000 homes throughout a number of Saudi Arabian cities by 2030. Developments by ROSHN, which is led by CEO David Grover, include residential units with high-tech insulation, solar-powered water heaters, and efficient air-conditioning systems – all surrounded by public spaces, green areas, and cycle pathways to promote a healthy lifestyle and walkable environment.

For these cities to be truly sustainable, they need to inspire future generations

These smarter models for urban design underpin PIF’s blueprint for the future of cities, but they do not define them. “We are very conscious of Saudi culture and heritage, and the way architecture varies around the kingdom,” explains Grover. “This means that while houses need to be high quality, they also need to be placed in a visual wrapper that fits in with the local architecture and the local community.”

ALAROUS will provide 18,000 new homes on the outskirts of Jeddah

Unique architectural styles are inspired by the region’s cultural heritage

The difference in architectural style between Jeddah and Riyadh, for example, reflects the different environmental and cultural conditions. “Everything from window height and style to the colour palette defines the architecture,” says Grover.

But PIF’s vision for the future of cities not only considers cultural traditions of the past, but also the citizens of the future.

ALAROUS will provide 18,000 new homes on the outskirts of Jeddah

Unique architectural styles are inspired by the region’s cultural heritage

THE LINE, for example, is being built for

9 million citizens

from different walks of life, each requiring specific services to fit around their lives. Meeting these needs requires technology-driven services that are able to collect individual data points and organise services around them.

These are not cities of the future, but cities of many futures. To maintain them over generations you need to invest in the people that are going to live and take pride in them for centuries to come.

To do this, urban centers are being built around iconic landmarks. In the north of Jeddah, an 11km-long,

100m-wide canal

will loop out from the Red Sea and provide a centrepiece for ROSHN’s new residential development of MARAFY.

New Murabba, meanwhile, will be built around the Mukaab, Riyadh’s iconic, cube-shaped building that will use giant holographic technology to create a constantly changing internal environment. “If you’re a young Saudi and you step inside a building like this, it’s going to inspire you to want to develop technology and be part of the future in some shape or form,” says Barakat. “This is not a city for 2035, 2040 or 2050,” he adds. Instead, this is a city for future generations, and for it to be sustainable it needs to be built around them.

If you’re a young Saudi and you step inside a building like this, it’s going to inspire you to want to develop technology and be part of the future in some shape or form

Sabah Barakat,
acting CEO of the New Murabba Development Company
